Frequently Asked Questions

Water filtration removes contaminants like bacteria, chemicals, and sediments from the water, while water softening specifically targets minerals like calcium and magnesium that cause water hardness. Filtration improves overall water quality, and softening prevents scale build-up.

The frequency of filter changes depends on the specific system and the capacity of the filters. Generally, carbon filters should be replaced every 3-6 months, while reverse osmosis membranes may last 2-3 years. Check the manufacturer's guidelines for more precise recommendations.

Yes, softened water is generally safe to drink. However, it may contain higher levels of sodium or potassium, depending on the softening process used. If sodium intake is a concern, consider using a potassium-based softener or retaining a separate filtered water line for drinking.

Water softeners can remove small amounts of ferrous iron (dissolved iron), but they are not designed to treat high iron concentrations. For significant iron removal, it is advisable to use a dedicated iron filtration system.

A reverse osmosis system forces water through a semi-permeable membrane, removing up to 99% of contaminants, including salts, bacteria, and chemicals. The process relies on pressure to overcome natural osmotic pressure, producing purified water while flushing away the impurities with wastewater.
